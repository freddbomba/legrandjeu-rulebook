What's different about LGJ

Before you run your first table

If you've run a D&D session, a Blades in the Dark heist, or any improv-heavy TTRPG, you already have most of the skills. But LGJ will ask you to suppress a few reflexes and develop others. Read this before your first game.

Reflex to suppress
The adversary posture
You are not the players' opponent. There is no BBEG. The world itself — its physics, scarcity, and consequences — is the opposition. Your job is to model it honestly, not to defeat the table.
Reflex to suppress
Plot-driven pacing
There is no story you are steering toward. The story is emergent; the round structure is the metronome. If nothing dramatic happens for two rounds, that's fine — the drama will come from the wheel, the economy, or an assembly.
Reflex to suppress
Rule-of-cool resolution
"That sounds awesome, roll for it" works in TTRPGs. In LGJ, awesome ideas still need a cost, a yield, and a grey footprint. Beauty is not an economic argument — but it can earn social value.

Reflexes to develop instead

Thinking in economics, not fiction

Every player proposal needs three numbers before it can be played: a construction cost (one-off), an operation cost (per round), and a yield (usually a dice expression). You will improvise these the way a D&D GM improvises DCs — fast, calibrated, with a table for reference.

Keeping the economy visible

Tokens on the table are not bookkeeping — they are the game. Players read the two collective pots, their own stacks, and each other's at a glance. Never resolve a transaction mentally; always move physical tokens. When in doubt, slow down and show the math.

Facilitating, not arbitrating

When a player invents something you've never seen — a carbon-credit market, a mutual-aid cooperative, a solar farm — don't rule from the chair. Ask the table: "Does this sound like it should cost more than a shop or less? Should it produce grey?" The group's collective knowledge is your calibration instrument.

The golden rule

"Yes, and how?"  — borrowed from improv theatre. Whatever a player proposes, your default is acceptance. The only question is how it enters the game: at what cost, with what yield, with what grey footprint, on or off the grid. Saying "no" is a last resort and almost always a sign you haven't yet asked the right "how".

The GM's three focus dials

During play, keep a part of your attention on three meters. Each has an opposite pole. Your job is to notice when one is drifting to an extreme and nudge back.

Stress
Challenged ↔ Empowered
Players should feel the economy bite, but never feel the game is rigged. If someone is visibly frustrated for two rounds, give them a cheap win or a fortune card.
Artist's block
Open ↔ Structured
Too much freedom and players freeze. Too much structure and they stop inventing. If ideas dry up, offer a concrete example. If ideas sprawl, ask for a cost.
Engagement
Participation ↔ Tempo
Ensure every player has spoken each round. If the loudest voice has dominated three turns, give the spotlight explicitly. Keep rounds under ~20 minutes.

Before the game

Session zero

Half the quality of a game is decided before any dice roll. Use 10–15 minutes at the top of the session to make these choices deliberately rather than by default.

Choose the scenario

A scenario is a situation that frames the session: the place, its resources, its stresses, its question. The SPES scenario asks "can we build a society inside planetary boundaries?". A textile-waste scenario asks "what do we do with the bales that keep arriving?". Choose one from the scenario pack, or write your own on a scenario card.

If this is the table's first game, prefer a simple, local scenario (a single bioregion, one river, no exotic technology). Complexity compounds fast.

Brief the players

Describe the world in two minutes: geography, population, climate, access to grid. Let players ask questions — but avoid over-specifying. Gaps are invitations for their invention.

Introduce the two currencies physically: hold up a white, hold up a grey, say what each means for this scenario (money/resources/knowledge vs pollution/depletion/waste). In an industrial scenario grey is mostly CO₂; in a rural one it might be soil erosion or water loss. Name it.

Set the governance rule

Flip a coin: heads = majority, tails = consensus. This determines how assemblies resolve. Consensus produces richer discussion but can stall; majority is faster but can exclude. Honour the coin — don't "re-flip until the one you wanted."

Tune the wheel

Default proportions are 50% LGJ (collective) / 20% Fortune (personal) / 15% Invent / 15% Destroy. The scenario may override this. A crisis scenario can go 70% LGJ. A "building peacetime" scenario might drop Destroy to 5%.

Hold the victory rule until asked

Don't announce how to win. Wait for the first player to ask. Who asks first — and what they assume victory means — tells you a lot about what this table wants from the game. Then reveal the rule, or ask the table to choose one of: individual score, collective survival, lowest-waste table.

Roll starting resources with the table watching

  1. Each player rolls 1d6 for starting territories and places them on or off the grid, narrating what the land is used for.
  2. Each player rolls 2d6 whites and 1d6 greys — the greys are starting baggage (legacy pollution, debts, accumulated impact), not a penalty.
  3. Each player names their starting activity. You write it on an element piece with cost and yield — see page 5 for calibration.
Facilitator note

If the table is diverse (language, experience, power), seed the starting positions yourself rather than by random roll — give the quieter players slightly more land, or the off-grid option they asked for. This is one of the places where the GM's discretion legitimately overrides the dice.

Explain the tax, then start

On-grid players pay 1 white per round into the local pot. This is visible, unavoidable, and frames the first question any new player asks: "should I go off-grid?" — which is the exact conversation the game wants to provoke.

Running the economy · part 1

Costing, yielding, calibrating

The core mechanical job. Every activity on the board is an element, and every element has four values. Your skill as GM is improvising those values fast, fairly, and in proportion to what's already on the table.

Element anatomy

Construction cost — whites paid once, when the element is first built. Inherited starting activities have cost zero.

Operation cost — whites and/or greys paid every round the element is active. Can be zero.

Yield — a dice expression. Player rolls each round they operate the element, receives that many whites. Some elements also produce greys as a by-product — note a grey coin next to the yield.

Calibration in four questions

When a player proposes a new activity, ask yourself (or the table):

  1. How hard is this to build? Hand-tool craft = 0–2 whites. Small shop = 2–4. Factory or hospital = 6–12. Major infrastructure = 15+. This is the construction cost.
  2. What does it consume every round it runs? A farm eats labour and water. A hospital burns energy and supplies. A festival consumes time but almost nothing material. This is the operation cost.
  3. What does it produce, and how reliably? Reliable, modest = d4. Normal, variable = d6 or 2d6. Large but risky = d10 or d20. This is the yield dice.
  4. Where does the grey come from? Burning anything, extracting anything, mass-producing anything. If the activity is net clean — gardening, teaching, performing — no grey. If ambiguous, mark 1d6: on a 1 it pollutes this round.
When in doubt, ask the table

"Does a community solar farm feel closer to a shop or a hospital?"   "Would you say a barter market produces grey?"   The table's consensus is not the truth, but it's a truth the game can run on. Write it down in the master's diary for next time.

The first round exception

In round one, each player's starting activity is inherited and pays no construction cost. They do, however, pay operation cost from round one onward. You still set a yield — typically d6 for a modest subsistence activity.

Handling "Invent"

When a player picks the Invent action, they're not deploying a ready activity — they're researching something that doesn't yet exist on the board. Process:

  1. Player describes the invention in 30 seconds.
  2. Table discusses for 60–90 seconds: is this plausible? What might it cost to research? What could it yield if it worked?
  3. You set a probability of success (a threshold on 1d20 — typically 10–15 for plausible ideas, higher for breakthroughs) and a research cost (3–6 whites plus 1–2 greys for lab waste is a fair baseline).
  4. Player pays. Rolls. On success, the invention becomes a new element with its own cost/yield/grey, which you write up fresh. On failure, the cost is lost but the knowledge is banked — next attempt gets a +2 bonus.

Facilitation craft

Reading the room

The numbers are a scaffold. The game happens in what players say between rolls — and your job is to make that conversation as productive as possible.

Prompting questions

When a player is stuck, don't suggest an activity. Ask a question that opens the space. A small library of prompts, grouped by what's stuck:

When someone can't think of what to build

  • What did your character do before the game started?
  • What does this bioregion need that no one is providing?
  • Who in your real life does something that could go on this board?
  • What would you build if you had twice the whites?

When the table is too individualistic

  • Whose activity depends on yours — directly, even if neither of you noticed?
  • What does this bioregion have that none of you own individually?
  • Is there anything it would be cheaper to build together?

When someone is about to do something destructive

  • Who else does this affect?
  • What would the land say if it could?
  • Is this a one-round decision or a permanent one?
  • What would you need to feel ok not doing this?

Spotlight management

Track who has spoken this round. If one player has dominated three actions, on the next round call on the quietest player first — not by embarrassing, but by routing: "You're closest to the river, do you see the spill from your land?" Giving them a concrete detail to respond to is easier than asking for an open contribution.

If two players start negotiating privately during another player's turn, don't stop them — but loop back: "Hold that thought; we'll come to it after this turn." Assemblies are where private negotiations become public.

Pacing

Each round should take 15–20 minutes in a 4-player game. If a round goes over 30, something is stuck — usually a proposal that can't be costed. Break the loop: write any number that feels fair, say "we'll revisit this in debrief", move on.

The wheel at end of round is a natural energy reset. Make it physical: get up, walk over, spin, return. The small interruption protects against the table getting lost in one player's spiral.

When to interrupt, when to let it play

Let bad ideas play. If a player wants to build a plastic-packaging megafactory next to the river, don't argue them out of it — stat it honestly, let the grey hit the table, and watch how the other players respond. The game teaches faster than you can.

Interrupt only when:

  • A player is about to violate a rule they didn't know existed (surprise "gotcha" is cruel).
  • The activity needs a crucial calibration check with the table (don't decide alone on a player's career-ending invention).
  • Someone at the table has gone visibly quiet in a way that suggests discomfort, not disengagement.
A tell worth learning

When a player starts explaining their idea in legal language — "am I allowed to?", "does the rule say I can?" — the game has drifted from improv to arbitration. Redirect: "Tell me what you want to do, not whether you can." The rules are for resolving outcomes, not for requesting permission.

Events, crisis & after

The wheel, the ceiling, the debrief

Reading events aloud

When the wheel lands on an LGJ or Fortune sector, don't read the card flatly. Read the title, pause, ask one player to describe what that would look like on this bioregion, then apply the mechanical effect. The card is a prompt, not a verdict.

For Invent and Destroy sectors, no card is drawn. You narrate an event that feels native to this scenario — a failed harvest, a rumour of a new technology, a fire in the next valley. Keep it short (30 seconds), let the table decide who is affected and how.

Modelling fire, pollution, crisis

Some events cascade. A pattern that works:

  • Fire — spin the wheel for wind direction. Downwind triangles roll d6: on 1–2 they ignite next round. An ignited triangle loses whatever is built on it. Put it out by surrounding it with whites (1 white per adjacent triangle).
  • Pollution plume — when grey in a region exceeds 10, adjacent territories lose d4 yield until it clears. Clears only by collective action.
  • Epidemic — each player rolls d10; on 1–3 loses d4 whites (productivity, healthcare costs). A hospital on the grid halves the damage.

When grey approaches 50

Don't hide the ecological ceiling from players. Announce it at 30, 40, 45. At 48, the table should be visibly nervous. If grey hits 50, end the game in narrative: describe the collapse, then stop.

A good session often does hit the ceiling. Don't treat this as a failure to prevent — it's the game doing its job.

When a player is about to die

If someone's about to run out of whites, let them know one round ahead: "If you don't earn 2 whites next round, your character is out." This is not saving them — it's giving them the chance to ask for help, call an assembly, or go off-grid. Elimination should never be a surprise.

If they do die: 5 grey per remaining round into the atmosphere pot. Narrate what they lose and what the bioregion loses with them. Invite them to stay as narrator / observer for the rest of the session.

After the game: 15 minutes of debrief

Every session gets at least a short debrief. Ask, in order:

  1. What did you build that you were proud of?
  2. What did the group get right? What did it miss?
  3. Did the wheel events feel real, contrived, or arbitrary?
  4. If we played this scenario again tomorrow, what would we do differently?
  5. What did this session tell you about the actual question the scenario asked?

Write the answers in the master's diary — both for next session, and because each table's answers reveal something the rules can't capture.

For workshops and research

If this session is part of a research or documentation context (see the SPES protocol), record the debrief with consent, photograph the final board state, note every custom rule invented at the table. These are the artefacts that make workshops reusable.

GM Cheatsheet · keep in front of you during play
The Golden Rule "Yes, and how?"

Round flow

  1. Pay tax — on-grid players: 1 to local pot.
  2. Each player acts — one of: Develop, Invent, Assembly, Learn, Enjoy.
  3. Spin the wheel — LGJ / Fortune / Invent / Destroy.
  4. Resolve event, move tokens visibly.
  5. Check ceilings: grey ≥ 50 ends the game.

The three dials

  • Stress — challenged ↔ empowered. Intervene if frustrated 2 rounds.
  • Artist's block — open ↔ structured. Offer example, or demand a cost.
  • Engagement — participation ↔ tempo. Every player speaks per round.

Setup checklist

  • Scenario chosen & briefed (2 min)
  • Currencies introduced physically
  • Coin flipped: majority or consensus?
  • Wheel tuned to scenario
  • Each player: 1d6 territory, 2d6, 1d6
  • Starting activity named & statted
  • Two pots (local + atmosphere) placed visibly
  • Victory rule held until first player asks

Ceilings & endings

  • Grey ≥ 50 → ecological collapse. Game ends.
  • Player out of whites → eliminated. +5 per remaining round.
  • Agreed rounds reached (usually 5–8).
  • Time up (1.5–2 hours).

Wheel sectors (default)

  • LGJ 50% — collective card, affects all.
  • Fortune 20% — personal card, one player.
  • Invent 15% — GM narrates a new possibility.
  • Destroy 15% — GM narrates a loss or setback.

Victory options

  • A · Individual — highest ( − ) wins.
  • B · Collective — survive ceiling, no one eliminated.
  • C · Tournament — table with least total grey wins.

Prompts when stuck

  • What does this bioregion need that no one is providing?
  • Whose activity depends on yours?
  • Who else does this affect?
  • What would the land say if it could?
  • Cost more or less than a shop? Than a hospital?

Do & don't

  • Do move tokens physically, always.
  • Do ask the table when you're unsure.
  • Do announce the ceiling as it approaches.
  • Don't argue players out of bad ideas — stat them honestly.
  • Don't surprise-eliminate a player.
  • Don't reveal victory until asked.
