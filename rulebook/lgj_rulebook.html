The Pieces & The Setup
2–7 players · 60–120 minutes · Ages 14+ · One Game Master

The Board

One board: a rhombus of 72 equilateral triangles in 12 rows, cut from A3. The scenario determines how many boards are needed — minimum one, often two joined side by side.

On the Grid

On-grid territories share infrastructure — roads, water, electricity, public services — paid for from the local pot. On-grid players pay tax each round.

Off the Grid

Place your triangles outside the board and you pay no tax — but you must provide everything you need yourself.

The Two Currencies

White

Money, resources, knowledge, energy, technology. Whites circulate through cost, yield and tax. Accumulating them is one possible goal — but not the only one.

Grey

Pollution, waste, depletion, entropy, bad karma. Grey is the cost the planet pays for your activity. You can reduce it, but never ignore it.

Score = Whites − Greys
Ecological Ceiling · max 50 grey

If the total grey in the bioregion (all players plus the atmosphere pot) exceeds 50, the game ends immediately: ecological collapse. No individual winner.

Death & Survival

If a player runs out of whites entirely, they are out of the game — and 5 grey tokens are added to the atmosphere pot per remaining round. Social abandonment always has a cost.

Setup

1
Appoint a Game Master. They run the economy, do not play as a citizen.
2
Each player picks a play-dough colour. More than 6 players? Form pairs.
3
Roll 1d6 for starting territories. Place on- or off-grid.
4
Roll 2d6 whites and 1d6 greys. Keep in front of you.
5
GM places the two collective pots: local money (whites) and atmosphere (greys).
6
Describe your starting activity. GM notes cost and yield on an element piece.
7
Flip a coin for governance: heads = majority, tails = consensus.

How to Play

A Round

Play proceeds round by round. Each round unfolds in three phases. A full game typically runs 5–8 rounds, ending by time, agreement, or ecological collapse.

Phase 1
Pay Tax
On-grid players put 1 white in the local pot. Off-grid players pay nothing.
Phase 2
Take Action
Each player, in turn, chooses one of the three action types below.
Phase 3
Spin the Wheel
One player spins the Wheel of Fortune. The GM narrates what happens.

The Five Actions

Develop
1d10

Describe what you want to build or improve on one of your territories. The GM estimates a cost and a yield. Roll 1d10 — the result shapes how well your venture starts. On the first round your starting activity is inherited at no build cost.

Invent
1d20

Propose a new idea — a technology, a process, a service. After brief group discussion the GM sets a probability of success and a research cost. Pay the cost, then roll 1d20. On success, gain a new functional element with its own cost and yield.

Assembly
vote

Any player may call an assembly to propose a collective decision — redistribute the tax pot, introduce a policy, change a rule, agree a shared project. Debate, then vote using the governance rule set during setup.

Learn
pay

Pay a cost (set by the GM) to acquire a skill or knowledge. Learning usually reduces future costs, unlocks new options, or opens doors that would otherwise be closed.

Enjoy
cost ⬜

Spend whites on pleasure, culture, celebration, rest. Enjoyment consumes grey tokens — a deliberate counterweight to pure production. It also strengthens social bonds, which the GM may reward in play.

Elements

Every activity on the board is an element. Each carries four values written on its piece: the one-off construction cost, the per-round operation cost, the yield dice, and a name.

The Game Master

The GM does not play against the players. Their golden rule — borrowed from improv — is "Yes, and how?" Whatever a player proposes, the GM helps turn it into a playable element: a cost, a yield, a grey-footprint.

They also spin the event wheel, read cards, moderate assemblies, run the two collective pots, and keep the economy visible at all times. When unsure about a value, the GM asks the table — the group's collective wisdom is the calibration tool.

Events, Victory & Reference

The Wheel of Fortune

LGJ — a collective event: policy shift, climate event, breakthrough, crisis. Affects everyone.

Fortune — a personal event: inheritance, inspector, local conflict. Affects one player.

Invent · Destroy — a creative or destructive impulse the GM shapes into a situation at the table.

Default proportions: 50% LGJ, 20% Fortune, 15% Invent, 15% Destroy. The GM may retune the wheel at the start to fit the scenario.

Victory

The victory rule is announced only when the first player asks how to win — what people ask first says something about what they expect.

Option A · Individual

The player with the highest score (whites minus greys) at the end wins.

Option B · Collective

The society wins if it stays within the ecological ceiling and no player has been eliminated.

Option C · Tournament

Across multiple tables, the table with the lowest total grey wins — least waste per white earned.

The game ends after the agreed number of rounds, fixed time (1.5–2 hrs), or when the ecological ceiling is breached.

Key Learnings

  • Carrying capacity & bioregion — atmosphere and land have hard limits.
  • Social impact — communal values, participation, who gets left behind.
  • Needs & consumption — costs, revenues, supply chains made visible.
  • Logistics — resilience and fragility of the network, trade dependencies.
